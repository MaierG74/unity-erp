## Workflow analysis

The prospect details in the brief are still incomplete: team size, current software stack, order volumes, SKU/variant count, outsourcing patterns, and named decision-makers were not provided, so the analysis below assumes a typical 50–300 employee mixed-process furniture manufacturer with short physical distance between the two plants and at least some make-to-order or configure-to-order behaviour. Those assumptions should be validated in the onboarding workshop before any price or ROI commitment is finalised.

The canonical operating model is straightforward on paper and messy in reality. A customer enquiry becomes a quote, then a sales order, then a production structure that must coordinate bought-out items, wood parts, upholstery materials, and fabricated steel components. At the steel site, the usual path is stock issue or kit pick, cutting/forming, welding, grinding and finishing, then inspection and transfer. At the wood/upholstery site, the path is panel or timber preparation, machining/joinery, sanding/finishing where relevant, foam/fabric cutting, sewing/upholstery, final assembly, inspection, packing, and dispatch. OSHA’s furniture and woodworking references reflect that these are genuinely different process families: wood furniture production involves machining and shaping operations such as turning, boring, and mortising, while upholstered furniture is classified separately because upholstery adds a distinct materials-and-labour stream on top of the wood frame. Finishing and adhesives introduce another control layer altogether. citeturn22search2turn22search3turn22search4turn22search5turn22search1

Steel fabrication diverges from wood/upholstery in ways that matter directly to ERP and agent design. Steel work is usually more routing-centric and operation-sequenced: cut, bend, weld, grind, finish, inspect, transfer. It often carries more setup/run logic, more shop discipline around machine/fixture sequencing, and more outbound dependency on whether a fabricated frame is dimensionally complete before downstream work can start. OSHA’s welding and metalworking guidance also underlines that the steel side has its own traceability and hazard profile around cutting, welding fumes, grinding, machining fluids, and hot work. That is exactly why steel jobs usually need clearer work-centre status, stronger exception flags, and better rework isolation than a generic “production complete / not complete” ERP status can provide. citeturn23search0turn23search1turn23search3turn23search8

Woodwork and upholstery diverge differently. The wood side tends to be more geometry- and variant-sensitive: dimensions, drilling patterns, nesting, cutlists, edge treatments, finishes, and joinery details. Upholstery adds fabric, foam, thread, trim, colour, texture, and defect sensitivity, plus more dependence on operator skill and visual quality. In many furniture businesses, the labour accounting logic also splits here: steel fabrication often tracks setup/run and rework by operation; upholstery often lends itself better to piecework, bundles, or earned-time logic. That matters because one labour model seldom fits both factories cleanly. Unity’s documented piecework/payroll mechanics and labour-planning board are a meaningful fit for this environment, but the pilot should assume that the steel site and the upholstery cells may need different job-card granularity and different rules for earned labour. fileciteturn18file0 fileciteturn19file0

The most valuable integration points between the bottom and top factories are fivefold. First, **component transfer**: when a steel frame or bracket leaves Site A, the receiving side must know exactly which order, revision, quantity, finish state, and defect status is arriving. Second, **schedule sync**: if the steel site slips by half a day, the upholstery or final assembly teams need the knock-on impact immediately, not by WhatsApp after lunch. Third, **quality handoff**: if a frame arrives twisted, scratched, short, or in the wrong revision, that must become a coded exception and not a verbal complaint. Fourth, **BOM roll-up**: the commercial order needs one truthful cost and availability picture across purchased components, steel subassemblies, wood parts, and upholstery materials. Fifth, **costing**: if steel labour is captured one way and upholstery another, the system still has to roll it into one order margin. Those are exactly the kinds of “central control” problems a manufacturing hub plus narrow, event-driven agents can solve better than a generic chatbot. fileciteturn17file0 fileciteturn22file0 fileciteturn25file0

Where small manufacturers in this segment usually lose money is rarely one dramatic issue; it is accumulation. They lose money in hidden WIP between buildings, mis-keyed receipts, duplicate buys because nobody trusts stock visibility, late discovery of shortages, unclear job priorities, unlogged rework, fabric/colour or component revision mismatches, and under-captured labour on short jobs. They also lose time in status chasing. APQC’s cross-industry median shows two days from requisition to PO release and 40 days from PO placement to completed pay cycle, which is a useful reminder that every manual handoff in procurement is expensive even before materials are late. APQC also shows median raw-material inventory turns of 10 and median WIP turns of 15 across large samples; those are not furniture-specific, but they give a sensible benchmark frame for spotting whether a two-site furniture plant is carrying too much avoidable raw stock or cross-site WIP. citeturn20search1turn20search2turn21search1turn21search4

For Unity specifically, the most compelling future-state picture is a planner/supervisor-facing control screen that unifies **queue, schedule, site transfer, receiving exceptions, labour availability, and blockers**. The repo’s own “Manufacturing Control Hub” recommendation points in exactly that direction. I would actively centre the proposal around that spine rather than around broader back-office ERP language. fileciteturn25file0

What is specifically defensible about Polygon’s **Telegram + Supabase MCP + multi-model** approach is not that it is more futuristic than the above. It is that it is **more practical for this exact operating context** if executed with discipline.

First, the interaction channel is realistic. Supervisors, buyers, stores staff, and founders in smaller manufacturers often live in messaging apps and phone cameras, not in polished copilots inside ten different browser tabs. A Telegram-first agent can meet the work where it already happens.

Second, the data path is tighter. The repo documents org-scoped RLS, read-only versus write-capable roles, edge-function writes, pending-action queues, and audit logging. That is a far stronger story than “ask AI anything” because it gives the buyer a believable answer to “who can do what, and how do we stop it from making a mess?” fileciteturn16file0 fileciteturn21file0 fileciteturn23file0

Third, the multi-model pattern is economically defensible. Using one model for reasoning and a cheaper/faster model for vision/OCR is not a gimmick; it is a sensible way to control latency and cost where the real value comes from combining document understanding, retrieval, and action. Glean is also now openly pushing broader LLM choice and interoperability, which suggests the market is moving towards this architecture rather than away from it. citeturn15search4

The caution is that this defence only holds if Polygon stays disciplined: bounded actions, visible audits, measurable workflows, and no premature “fully autonomous factory PA” pitch.

## Pilot plan

A 90-day pilot should be sold as **Control System Pilot**, not an ERP big-bang. The point is to prove that both sites can run from the same operational truth and that 2–3 agents can remove friction without creating fear.

| Week | Focus | Deliverables |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Onboarding workshop | Process walk-through at both sites; order-to-ship map; current system map; roles/approvals; pilot KPIs agreed; baseline measurements started |
| 2 | Data scope lock | Pilot item master subset; suppliers; open POs; open jobs; transfer logic; site/location model; user/role matrix |
| 3 | Environment and permissions | Org/site setup, RLS checks, read-only and write-capable service roles, audit trail and pending-action design confirmed fileciteturn16file0 fileciteturn21file0 |
| 4 | Receiving workflow | Delivery-note intake, PO matching rules, discrepancy states, receiving review queue |
| 5 | Inter-site transfer workflow | Transfer request, dispatch, receipt, damage/shortage coding, order/job linkage |
| 6 | Exception model | Shared blocker taxonomy for both sites: shortage, awaiting transfer, quality hold, labour unavailable, supplier late, engineering clarification |
| 7 | Agent launch one | Receiving & PO Match Agent live for pilot suppliers/orders |
| 8 | Agent launch two | Inter-Site Transfer Agent live for steel-to-top-factory movements |
| 9 | Control hub prototype | Daily view for late receipts, stuck transfers, blocked jobs, and urgent customer orders; routed to supervisors and owner |
| 10 | Agent launch three | Production Exception Triage Agent live; daily digest to Telegram |
| 11 | User hardening | Tune prompts, false-positive thresholds, escalation logic, exception ownership, and SOPs |
| 12 | Pilot review | Compare baseline vs current, document time saved, failure modes, and adoption data |
| 13 | Go/no-go pack | Decision memo, rollout backlog, phase-2 candidates, and confirmed non-goals |

The **data-migration scope** for the pilot should be intentionally narrow: active suppliers, open purchase orders, active SKUs/materials for the pilot families, current stock positions for pilot locations, open jobs/orders, site/location structure, and user/role data. Do **not** attempt full historical migration in 90 days. The pilot does not need perfect history; it needs accurate live control.

The first agents to ship should be **Receiving & PO Match**, **Inter-Site Transfer**, and **Production Exception Triage**. They are first because they solve the prospect’s actual central-control problem, create auditable ERP events, and minimise political risk. I would delay customer-facing and payroll-facing agents until internal trust is established.

The pilot success criteria should be measurable and boring in the best possible way. I would use five. First, at least **85%** of pilot receiving documents should be auto-classified and matched strongly enough that a human can finalise them inside a short review step rather than re-keying whole receipts. Second, at least **90%** of pilot inter-site movements should be digitally logged and acknowledged the same shift. Third, both supervisors should use the daily exception digest at least **five days per week**. Fourth, the baseline group of planners/buyers/supervisors should report at least **25% less manual status-chasing time** on the pilot families. Fifth, every agent write/action should be auditable and reversible. The repo’s control patterns are a good technical base for that standard. fileciteturn21file0 fileciteturn23file0

What should be hidden from the client until phase 2 is just as important. Keep **autonomous document sending, auto-approval of purchases, payroll-affecting writebacks, customer-facing ETA promises, marketing/flyer generation, and any broad multi-agent orchestration** out of the pilot story. They distract from the control-system thesis and increase perceived risk faster than they increase value.

## Full rollout

If the pilot lands, the next 12 months should follow dependency order rather than wishful parallelism.

| Phase | What goes live | Why this order matters |
|---|---|---|
| Months 1–3 | Core item/site/order backbone; receiving; purchasing; inter-site transfers; exception taxonomy; control-hub dashboards; daily digests | Without truthful events and statuses, later ETA, replenishment, and customer-facing agents will hallucinate over bad data |
| Months 4–6 | Job-card discipline; labour capture; piecework/timekeeping stabilisation; payroll anomaly checks; quality/NCR capture; rework visibility | Costing, scheduling credibility, and wage trust depend on reliable labour and reject data |
| Months 7–9 | Better costing; quote drafting; order-status responder; replenishment advisor; more complete work-pool and finite labour planning | Only after operations are being recorded cleanly should the business automate promises and recommendations |
| Months 10–12 | Configurator-to-production improvements where validated; executive briefings; supplier chase automation; broader agent orchestration; external integrations; phase-2 experiments | Advanced autonomy and design-to-make flows should come last, because they depend on clean templates, routings, and governance |

Two important rollout caveats should be explicit in the proposal. ## ROI model

This model is intentionally conservative and uses a mix of published benchmarks and explicit scenario assumptions. The published anchors are: Stats SA’s average monthly formal non-agricultural earnings of **R29,690** in late 2025; APQC’s median **2.0 days** from requisition to PO release; APQC’s median **40.0 days** procure-to-pay cycle; APQC’s median raw-material inventory turns of **10.0**; and APQC’s median WIP turns of **15.0**. Rockwell/Plex’s 2025 smart manufacturing survey also shows how aggressively manufacturers are now evaluating AI and digital operations: more than **1,500** manufacturers were surveyed, **95%** had invested or planned to invest in AI-related technologies within five years, **56%** were piloting smart manufacturing, and only **20%** said they were using it at scale. citeturn19search1turn20search1turn20search2turn21search1turn21search4turn19search0

Using the Stats SA average monthly earnings as a rough blended labour-value proxy implies around **R171 per labour hour** before any higher loaded-cost assumption is added. That is a blunt instrument, but acceptable for a board-level first pass. citeturn19search1

| ROI lever | Conservative pilot/rollout assumption | Annualised effect |
|---|---|---|
| Admin and coordination hours saved | 25–40 hours/week removed across receiving, status chasing, handover, transfer reconciliation, and exception follow-up | 1,300–2,080 hours/year |
| Labour-value equivalent | Using ~R171/hour proxy from Stats SA AME | ~R222k–R356k/year |
| Internal procurement cycle reduction | Use agents to cut avoidable internal PO/RFQ/admin delay by 0.5–1.0 day on pilot categories against APQC’s 2-day median PO release benchmark | Faster material commit, less expediting risk; financial value depends on baseline lateness |
| Inventory and WIP control | If client is below APQC medians of 10 raw-material turns and 15 WIP turns, better cross-site visibility should reduce duplicated buys and stranded WIP | Working-capital release is highly baseline-dependent; every R1m avoided in unnecessary stock frees R1m cash |
| Payroll exception reduction | Scenario assumption: 30–50% fewer manually corrected anomalies after stable timekeeping/piecework capture | Small direct cash effect; meaningful supervisor/payroll time recovery and reduced disputes |
| Quote-to-response improvement | Scenario assumption: 1–2 working days faster on repeat/configurable quotes once costing and data discipline are stable | Commercial upside depends on sales volume and current hit rate |
| Quality/rework reduction | Scenario assumption: 5–10% fewer unlogged or late-detected defects on pilot families through coded handoff and photo capture | Often worth more in on-time delivery and credibility than in pure scrap cash during pilot |

My conservative read is that the **hardest, most defensible first-year value** will come from the first row alone: saved coordination/admin time, fewer missed receipts/transfers, and less daily firefighting. On that basis, the first-year labour-value case alone can plausibly sit in the **R220k–R350k** range before adding any working-capital or service-level benefits. Material- and inventory-side upside is real, but it should be sold with formulas, not with invented certainty.

In terms of cycle economics, the inventory-turn benchmarks make the case for urgency. A raw-material turn rate of 10 implies about **36.5 days** of raw-stock coverage; a WIP turn rate of 15 implies about **24.3 days** of WIP equivalence. For a two-site furniture manufacturer, even shaving **2–3 days** of cross-site waiting, unclear transfer ownership, or blocker latency is significant. That is why the central-control spine matters more than a flashy front-end assistant. citeturn21search1turn21search4

I would therefore present ROI in three layers to the client: **hard labour-value**, **working-capital/control improvement**, and **commercial responsiveness**. The first is measurable inside the pilot; the second becomes measurable after the transfer and receiving disciplines stabilise; the third should wait until data quality on costs and routing is trusted.

## Risks and open questions

The five biggest rollout risks are organisational as much as technical.

**Master-data weakness** is the first. If item masters, BOMs, spend units, supplier lead times, and route definitions are inconsistent, agents will only automate confusion. This is especially important for any later configurator or replenishment promise. The repo itself is candid that some manufacturing-control and configurator areas are still evolving. fileciteturn20file0 fileciteturn25file0

**Poor event discipline on the shop floor** is the second. A central-control system only works if receipts, transfers, issues, rejects, and blockers are actually recorded. If staff keep using side-channel calls and memory, the control screen becomes theatre.

**Overextending autonomy too early** is the third. It is tempting to show fully autonomous actions because Matt demos well. For this prospect, that would be a mistake in phase 1. Autonomous writes touching payroll, supplier commitments, or customer promises should only follow after trust, audit, and exception ownership are established. The OpenClaw/Supabase architecture supports that kind of control if used properly. fileciteturn21file0 fileciteturn23file0

**Channel fit risk** is the fourth. Telegram may be excellent for founders and supervisors and mediocre for everyone else. The pilot needs to test whether the operational users prefer Telegram, mobile web, tablet kiosk, or a mix.

**Commercial overpromising** is the fifth. Epicor, Salesforce, Glean, Microsoft, Infor, and Fishbowl are all now promising agents. The market is noisy. Polygon should not try to win by sounding more magical than they do; it should win by being more concrete, more auditable, and more furniture-specific. citeturn14search2turn16search2turn15search2turn17search1turn18search3turn18search9

The three most important things to learn during the pilot before promising more are these. First, **what is the real current-system map?** Spreadsheet stack, accounting package, paper traveller usage, barcode capability, and how much of the real plan lives outside the current system. Second, **what is the true inter-site delay pattern?** How often do steel components move late, incomplete, or wrong-revision, and how much downstream time do they burn? Third, **how mature is the commercial master data?** Variant rules, cutlist reliability, route accuracy, and whether the current configurator can be trusted beyond cutlist generation for this customer’s product mix.

**Open questions / limitations**

This report could not responsibly fill the following prospect-specific fields because they were not provided in the brief: actual employee count, current systems, order/SKU volume, outsource finishing steps, current inventory accuracy, baseline payroll dispute rate, and the decision-maker map. In addition, several analyst datasets the brief referenced by name, especially deeper Gartner/Aberdeen/IDC numeric benchmarks, are not publicly accessible in enough detail to use as defensible quantitative evidence here. I therefore grounded the model in repo documentation, official vendor pages, APQC public benchmark pages, Rockwell/Plex public survey findings, and Stats SA public data, and marked scenario assumptions separately where no publishable benchmark was strong enough.
