1 · The core question — when is a product done?

One cupboard does not equal one job card. A cupboard moves through three workstations:

A cupboard = 3 job cards across 3 sections. A steel table = fabrication + powdercoating = 2 job cards.

So "is the product finished?" can only be answered by looking at all of its job cards together. One card finishing means a stage is done, not the whole product.

2 · What the system does today

Honestly: the foundation is half built, and there's no aggregate "done" signal anywhere.

What exists

  • manufacturing_sections — lookup table (Cutting, Edging, Assembly, Powdercoating…)
  • order_manufacturing_sections — one row per order × section, with started_at and completed_at columns
  • job_cards.status can be 'completed'
  • job_card_items.status can be 'completed'
  • product_inventory — finished-goods stock table, with QOH + transactions
  • products.is_stocked boolean — flags which products are tracked in stock

What's missing

  • No per-product routing — nothing tells the system "a cupboard needs Cutting+Edging+Assembly"
  • No section_id on job_cards — cards aren't linked to their stage
  • Nothing populates order_manufacturing_sections.completed_at
  • No cascade — when all sections finish, nothing fires
  • No "order is done" status flip — orders can sit at "In Production" indefinitely
  • No delivery-note concept, anywhere
  • No internal-order concept, anywhere
Implication. If we want a reliable "product finished" event — for stock check-in or for closing customer orders — we have to introduce one. Anything downstream (stock-in, delivery notes, order closure) depends on it.

3 · A reliable "ready" signal we can build on

We have two choices. They're not equivalent.

Option A · Section cascade

Build per-product section routing

Add a product_sections join table per product, populate order_manufacturing_sections.completed_at when a job card representing that section finishes, fire a "product done" event when the last section closes.

  • Architecturally clean
  • ~3× the work of Option B
  • Touches customer orders too (RLS, migrations, UI)
  • Requires deciding routing per product — every product gets configured
Option B · Job-card rollup

Roll up the signals we already have

A product (an order_items row) is "ready" when every job_card_items row referencing it has status='completed'. We don't need to know which sections were involved — we just need to know all the cards finished.

  • Uses signals that already exist and are well-defined
  • No new per-product configuration burden
  • Doesn't preclude adding the section model later
  • Simpler to reason about, easier to audit
Recommendation. Start with Option B (job-card rollup). It gives us a "ready" event today, with no new per-product configuration. We can layer Option A on later if/when we need section-level reporting.

What the "ready" event looks like

All job cards on an order_item complete → item flips to "ready" → fork based on order_type.

4 · The fork: stock vs delivery note

Same upstream pipeline. Two destinations. The order's order_type decides which one.

Internal order

→ Finished-goods stock

When an item flips to "ready", we either auto-write to product_inventory_transactions (silent) or flag the order "ready to receive" and a stock user clicks once to confirm.

Recommendation: the one-click variant. It costs almost nothing and gives an audit trail ("Sue received 5 cupboards at 14:32"). Auto-magic stock changes are the thing that bites you six months later.

Customer order

→ Delivery note

Ready items accumulate in a "ready to ship" bucket on the order. A user creates a delivery note that pulls some or all of them, prints, and the items get marked "delivered" on that note's date.

Why this matters: there's no way to close an order properly today. A delivery note is how a customer order closes — fully when all items are delivered, partially when some are.

5 · Delivery notes & partial fulfilment

Your example: customer orders 100 cupboards, you deliver 50 now, 50 next week. Two delivery notes.

An order closes when the sum of delivered quantities across all its delivery notes equals the ordered quantities.

Shape of a delivery note

  • delivery_notes — one row per note: order_id, note_number (e.g. DN-0001), delivery_date, signed_by, signed_at, status (draft / printed / signed / cancelled), org_id, notes
  • delivery_note_items — one row per line: delivery_note_id, order_item_id, quantity
  • Constraint: ∑ delivery_note_items.quantity per order_item_id ≤ order_items.quantity. You can't deliver more than was ordered.
  • "Ready to ship" available count = item's ready_qty minus quantity already on signed delivery notes.
  • Order status 'Closed' = every order_items.quantity fully delivered across signed notes. Could be automatic on signing the final note.
The same data model works for internal orders, with a tweak: instead of a delivery note, an internal order produces a stock-receipt note (same shape, different destination — pushes into product_inventory_transactions). Same UX, same partial-fulfilment behaviour, just a different downstream side-effect. We could literally use the same table with a note_type column, or fork into two tables — open question for you.

6 · The full picture

Customer + internal orders share the manufacturing pipeline. They differ only at the "ready" fork.

7 · Open questions for you

Before I write the design doc, a handful of branch points where I'd like your call.

Q1 — One notes table or two?
Delivery notes (to customer) and stock-receipt notes (to stock) have nearly identical shape. Option (a): single fulfilment_notes table with a note_type column. Option (b): two separate tables, delivery_notes and stock_receipts. Option (a) is less duplication; option (b) is clearer in queries and reports.
Q2 — Auto-close orders, or manual?
When every line on a customer order is fully delivered, do we auto-flip the order to 'Closed', or just show "Ready to close" and let an admin confirm? Auto is cleaner; manual gives one last check.
Q3 — Reservation against internal orders?
Customer orders today have product_reservations that hold stock for a specific order. Should an internal order ever consume stock as raw input (e.g. building a finished product that uses an already-stocked sub-product), or are internal orders always net producers of stock?
Q4 — Sections model: now, later, or never?
The job-card rollup gives us "product done" without building per-product section routing. But the section infrastructure is half-built. Do we leave it dormant for now, or fold "populate order_manufacturing_sections.completed_at" into this work so the table actually carries data?
Q5 — Delivery-note numbering and PDF
Sequence per org (DN-0001, DN-0002…) is the obvious choice. Should the note generate a printable PDF on creation (one click → printable note + signature line)? Or keep it data-only for v1 and add the PDF later?
Q6 — Where does the "stock receipts" history live?
A page under inventory showing "what came into stock from internal orders, when, by whom"? Or just rows in product_inventory_transactions with a filter? The former is easier to use; the latter is zero extra UI work.
